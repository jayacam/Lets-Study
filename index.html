PED6: The Child and Adolescent Learners and Learning Principles – Comprehensive In-Depth Discussion

This course (often coded as PED 6, EDUC 1, ED 101, or similar in Philippine teacher-education programs under CHED and aligned with the K-12 curriculum and RA 10533) forms a foundational pillar for pre-service teachers. It equips future educators with a deep, research-based understanding of how children (typically ages 0–12) and adolescents (13–18/19) develop physically, cognitively, socially, emotionally, and morally — and how these processes directly shape effective teaching and learning. The coverage integrates developmental psychology, learning theories, learner-centered principles, individual differences, and practical classroom applications. It emphasizes that teaching is not just content delivery but facilitating holistic growth in diverse, real-world contexts, including inclusive settings.

The course typically spans 18 weeks (3 units/credits), with midterms and finals assessing theory application. Core objectives include:
- Demonstrating understanding of child/adolescent nature, theories, principles, and factors in learning.
- Applying developmental knowledge to build positive learner relationships and select appropriate strategies/materials.
- Analyzing motivation, reasoning, problem-solving, and assessment.
- Advocating learner-centered, inclusive practices.

Below is an exhaustive, in-depth discussion of all standard coverage, synthesized from typical syllabi, modules, and resources used in Philippine institutions (e.g., Cavite State University, Silliman, Philippine Normal University, and common modules).

1. Defining the Child and Adolescent Learner

Core Definitions
Section Content Child • Adolescent • Key distinctions • Implications
›
- Child: Generally birth to puberty (0–12 years). Per UN Convention on the Rights of the Child and Philippine contexts (e.g., RA 7610, DepEd policies), a child is under 18, but educationally, focus is on early/middle childhood where foundational skills form. Characteristics: Rapid physical growth, concrete thinking, egocentrism decreasing, dependence on adults transitioning to peer influence.
- Adolescent: Puberty to adulthood (13–18/19+). Marked by identity formation, abstract thinking, emotional volatility, and preparation for independence. Philippine legislation (e.g., RA 8044 Youth Council) views adolescence as a critical transition period.
- Key Distinctions: Children rely on sensory/concrete experiences; adolescents handle hypotheticals, moral dilemmas, and self-concept shifts. Teachers must differentiate instruction accordingly (e.g., hands-on for children, debate/project-based for adolescents).

Implications: Every learner is unique; teachers must observe developmental readiness, not just chronological age.

2. Fundamental Concepts: Growth, Development, Maturation, and Learning

Core Concepts + Principles
Key Definitions Growth • Development • Maturation • Learning
›
- Growth: Quantitative, measurable physical changes (height, weight, brain size). Predictable but influenced by nutrition/genetics.
- Development: Qualitative, progressive increase in complexity/function (e.g., from crawling to walking; simple to abstract thought). Lifelong, multidimensional (physical, cognitive, socioemotional).
- Maturation: Genetically driven, age-related unfolding of traits (e.g., puberty). Readying for learning but requires environmental stimulation.
- Learning: Relatively permanent change in behavior/knowledge from experience, interaction, or instruction. Not just maturation; involves active construction.
Major Principles of Development Orderly • Varying rates • Holistic • Plasticity • Lifelong
›
Major Principles of Development (widely covered):
1. Development is orderly/sequential (cephalocaudal: head-to-toe; proximodistal: center-to-extremities).
2. Proceeds at varying rates (spurts in infancy/puberty; plateaus in middle childhood).
3. Continuous yet staged; influenced by heredity (nature) and environment (nurture) interaction.
4. Critical/sensitive periods exist (e.g., language acquisition).
5. Holistic: Domains interrelate (physical health affects cognition).
6. Plasticity: Can be modified by experiences.
7. Individual variation; no two develop identically.
8. Lifelong and contextual (Bronfenbrenner influence).
Approaches to Development Traditional • Lifespan
›
Approaches to Development:
- Traditional: Heavy emphasis birth–adolescence; decline in old age.
- Lifespan: Change continues across life; multidimensional, plastic, contextual, gradual/cumulative.
Havighurst’s Developmental Tasks Milestones across stages
›
Havighurst’s Developmental Tasks (age-specific milestones; failure leads to unhappiness/difficulty in later tasks):

- Infancy/Early Childhood (0–5/6): Walking, talking, toilet training, forming concepts, distinguishing right/wrong, emotional attachment.
- Middle/Late Childhood (6–12): Physical skills, wholesome peer relations, gender roles, reading/writing/arithmetic, conscience, personal independence, attitudes toward groups.
- Adolescence (13–18): Emotional independence from adults, economic/career preparation, mature gender roles, peer relations, civic responsibility, value system.

Educational implication: Align curriculum to tasks (e.g., cooperative learning for peer relations in middle childhood).

3. Major Theories of Child and Adolescent Development

Theories
Cognitive Development – Jean Piaget Stages • Assimilation/Accommodation • Teaching
›
Stages emphasize how children construct knowledge through assimilation/accommodation and equilibrium.

- Sensorimotor (0–2): Object permanence, sensory-motor coordination.
- Preoperational (2–7): Symbolic thought, language, but egocentrism, centration, lack of conservation.
- Concrete Operational (7–11): Logical operations on concrete objects (conservation, classification, seriation).
- Formal Operational (12+): Abstract, hypothetical-deductive reasoning, moral/ideological thinking.

Criticism: Underestimates social/cultural influences; stages not rigid.
Teaching: Provide age-appropriate challenges (scaffolding for next stage); use manipulatives for concrete operational learners.
Psychosocial Development – Erik Erikson Stages • Virtues • School-age/adolescent focus
›
Crisis-resolution builds virtues/strengths.

- Trust vs. Mistrust (0–1): Hope.
- Autonomy vs. Shame/Doubt (1–3): Will.
- Initiative vs. Guilt (3–6): Purpose.
- Industry vs. Inferiority (6–12): Competence (key for school success).
- Identity vs. Role Confusion (12–18): Fidelity (adolescent focus; moratorium/exploration).
- Later: Intimacy, Generativity, Integrity.

Implications: Support competence in middle childhood (praise effort); facilitate identity exploration in adolescence (career guidance, clubs) to avoid confusion.
Socio-Cultural Theory – Lev Vygotsky ZPD • Scaffolding • MKO • Culture
›
- Social interaction drives development (Zone of Proximal Development – ZPD: tasks child can do with guidance but not alone).
- Scaffolding, private speech, cultural tools (language).
- More knowledgeable Other (MKO) crucial.

Implications: Collaborative learning, peer tutoring, guided discovery. Culture shapes cognition (Philippine emphasis on bayanihan/group work fits perfectly).
Ecological Systems Theory – Urie Bronfenbrenner Nested systems • Classroom implications
›
Development in nested systems.

- Microsystem: Immediate (family, school, peers).
- Mesosystem: Interactions between micros (home-school).
- Exosystem: Indirect (parent’s workplace).
- Macrosystem: Culture/society/values.
- Chronosystem: Time/transitions.

Implications: Teachers influence micro/mesosystems; address poverty/family issues holistically (e.g., parent involvement programs).
Moral Development – Lawrence Kohlberg Levels • Criticism • Teaching
›
- Preconventional: Punishment/reward.
- Conventional: Approval/law-and-order.
- Postconventional: Social contract/universal ethics.

Criticism: Gender/cultural bias (Gilligan’s care ethics alternative).
Teaching: Dilemma discussions to advance reasoning.

Other theories briefly: Freud (psychosexual – early influences), Bandura (social learning – modeling), Maslow (humanistic needs hierarchy).

6. Learning Theories & Principles

Learning Models
Section Content Behavioral • Social Cognitive • Cognitive • Constructivist
›
- Behavioral: Thorndike (law of effect), Pavlov (classical), Skinner (operant – reinforcement), applied behavior analysis.
- Social Cognitive: Bandura (observational learning, self-efficacy).
- Cognitive/Information Processing: Attention → Memory (sensory, short-term, long-term) → Metacognition; Gestalt (insight); Ausubel (advance organizers); Bruner (spiral curriculum, discovery); Gagné (conditions/hierarchies).
- Constructivist: Piaget/Vygotsky extensions; meaningful, active building of knowledge.
- Complex processes: Problem-solving, transfer, critical/creative thinking.

Implications: Use reinforcement positively; model behaviors; scaffold; promote metacognition (think-alouds).
